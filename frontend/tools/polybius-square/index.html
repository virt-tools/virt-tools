Polybius Square

Encrypt and decrypt with the Polybius Square, a classical substitution cipher that maps each letter of the alphabet to a pair of coordinates on a 5×5 grid. Invented by Polybius in ancient Greece, it replaces letters with numbers, effectively converting text into a numeric code. The standard square merges I and J into a single cell to fit 26 letters into 25 slots. By changing the alphabet order or using different grid dimensions, the square can be adapted for various purposes. Enter text to encipher or numbers to decipher, with the active grid shown for reference. Everything runs locally in your browser.

5×5 Square

The math. The square is filled by placing the keyword (upper-cased, J→I, duplicates removed) in the first available cells, followed by the rest of the alphabet ABCDEFGHIKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ in order. To encrypt, each letter is replaced by its (row, column) coordinates (e.g., if 'H' is at row 2, col 3, it becomes 23). To decrypt, pairs of digits are mapped back to their corresponding letter in the grid. Spaces and non-alphabetic characters are preserved during encryption. Since I and J share a cell, they are treated as the same letter during both processes. Pairs with the Caesar, Vigenère, Playfair, and other Classical Ciphers tools.
