Autokey Cipher

Encrypt and decrypt with the autokey cipher, a polyalphabetic cipher (also called the autoclave or Vigenère autokey) that fixes the great weakness of the repeating-key Vigenère cipher. Instead of looping a short keyword, the autokey builds a keystream that never repeats: it starts with a short primer (the keyword) and then continues with the plaintext itself. So each plaintext letter is shifted by the keyword's letters for the first stretch, then by the earlier plaintext letters. Because the key grows with the message, no short cycle exists for frequency analysis to exploit — a fact recognized by Vigenère himself in 1586, though the cipher was largely forgotten until the 19th century. Encryption is C = (P + K) mod 26; decryption reverses it, recovering each plaintext letter from the primer (for the first letters) and then from plaintext it has already decoded. Case and non-letters pass through untouched, and the key advances only on letters. Enter a primer and text to encipher or decipher, with the keystream shown letter-by-letter. Everything runs locally in your browser.

Keystream breakdown (encrypt)

The math. The autokey cipher is a polyalphabetic substitution: each letter P at position i is shifted by a keystream letter K[i], giving C[i] = (P[i] + K[i]) mod 26 (A=0 … Z=25). The keystream is the primer (the keyword, upper-cased, letters only) followed by the plaintext letters themselves: K = primer ‖ P. For the first m letters (where m is the primer length) the shift comes from the primer; from position m onward each shift is the plaintext letter m positions back — so K[i] = P[i − m]. This is what makes the key effectively as long as the message and non-repeating, defeating the Kasiski / index-of-coincidence attacks that break Vigenère. Decryption inverts it: P[i] = (C[i] − K[i]) mod 26, where the keystream is the primer followed by the plaintext already recovered, so decoding progressively reconstructs the key from its own output. Case is preserved and non-letter characters (spaces, digits, punctuation) pass through untouched and do not advance the keystream. Pairs with the Vigenère, Beaufort, Caesar, and Classical Ciphers tools. Everything runs locally — nothing leaves your browser.
